Executive Summary

Working in the secondary market space, the core task was always the same: identify what people want, source it before the broader market catches on, and move it at the right time. Doing that consistently required understanding why prices move, not just tracking them. This report synthesizes the economic research and market analysis that informed those sourcing decisions across GPUs, Consoles, & Collectibles. The 2020–2022 period created some of the most volatile secondary market conditions in recent history — driven by government stimulus, a crypto mining boom, and global chip shortages — and understanding that backdrop was essential to making sense of where prices stood in 2023 and where they were heading.

01 — Macroeconomic Context
How Stimulus Money Turbocharged Consumer Electronics Demand

Between March 2020 and March 2021, the U.S. government issued three rounds of direct stimulus payments totaling up to $3,200 per eligible adult, alongside the Paycheck Protection Program (PPP) which injected over $800 billion into the economy. With lockdowns in place, physical spending curtailed, and remote work driving the need for upgraded home setups, a significant portion of this liquidity flowed directly into consumer electronics. Simultaneously, cryptocurrency prices surged, with Bitcoin reaching $69,000 in November 2021 and Ethereum peaking above $4,800. Because Ethereum used an ASIC-resistant mining algorithm, GPUs remained the only viable mining hardware, creating a second massive demand channel for graphics cards that distribution networks were never designed to handle. At the height of the boom, crypto miners accounted for an estimated 25% of all GPU purchases in the first half of 2021, according to Jon Peddie Research.

02 — Monitoring Methodology
How Demand Signals Were Tracked in Real Time

The research approach combined macroeconomic analysis with field-level observation, using economic indicators to establish the broader demand context, then validating those signals through direct vendor engagement and consumer behavior observed at live events. This dual-layer methodology allowed for higher-confidence sourcing decisions, as quantitative models were tested against real-world conditions rather than applied in isolation.

Field Research & Event Intelligence
Attended trade shows, pop-ups, and vendor events to observe consumer behavior firsthand and build sourcing relationships. On-the-ground insights surfaced demand signals that quantitative data alone could not capture.
Econometric Demand Modeling
Applied economic indicators, CPI, inflation rate, real consumer spending, and crypto asset prices, to model demand trajectories across product categories. Correlated macro shifts with observed secondary market pricing to identify early signals.
Vendor & Supply Chain Intelligence
Tracked distribution constraints and vendor network capacity to anticipate supply gaps. Vendor relationships built at events provided advance visibility into share timelines and restock windows before they became publicly known.
Data Synthesis & Strategic Reporting
Synthesized vendor data, pricing trends, and economic indicators into structured reports used for investment and sourcing decisions. Translated raw market observations into actionable recommendations with results that could be tracked.
Web Infrastructure & Inventory Monitoring
Applied working knowledge of retail backend systems, request routing, and inventory endpoint behavior to monitor stock availability at scale. Configured proxy and ISP networks to distribute monitoring across retailer infrastructure, enabling real-time visibility into restock events across multiple channels at the same timely.
03 — NVIDIA GPU Market
The Mining-Driven Shortage & Post-Crash Correction

GPU demand from cryptocurrency mining is not new — it traces back to Bitcoin's early days in 2013, when consumer graphics cards were the primary mining hardware. That changed when custom ASICs replaced GPUs for Bitcoin, making general-purpose cards unprofitable for BTC mining. The cycle restarted with Ethereum, which was intentionally designed with an ASIC-resistant mining algorithm, making GPUs the only viable mining hardware again. This design choice is what made the 2020–2022 shortage so severe: every GPU sold was competing between two entirely separate demand pools — gamers and miners — with no dedicated supply chain for either.

NVIDIA's RTX 30-series launched in September 2020 at $699 MSRP (RTX 3080). According to research from Jon Peddie Research, crypto miners purchased an estimated 25% of all GPUs produced in the first half of 2021. With pandemic-related chip shortages shrinking supply at the same timely, secondary market prices reached 3x retail or higher by February 2021. The feedback loop of the shortage made it worse: miners had little incentive to sell cards that were actively generating returns, further restricting secondary market supply. When Ethereum transitioned to proof-of-stake in September 2022, eliminating GPU mining profitability overnight, miners began liquidating their entire fleets at the same timely. Combined with the RTX 40-series launch, the 30-series overcorrected below MSRP by early 2023 — creating a clear re-entry window before gaming demand stabilized prices.

RTX 3080, Secondary Market Price vs. MSRP (2020–2023)
Key Driver: Dual Demand Pool
Ethereum's GPU-only mining design made GPUs the only viable mining hardware, putting gamers and miners in direct competition for the same supply. At peak ETH prices, a single RTX 3080 could generate $8–12/day in mining revenue, making miners unwilling to sell even at 3x retail markup.
Signal: Post-Merge Liquidation Window
Ethereum's shift to proof-of-stake in September 2022 made GPU mining instantly unprofitable. The resulting mass liquidation of mining fleets pushed 30-series prices 10–20% below MSRP by Q1 2023, a short-term pricing gap before organic gaming demand restored normal pricing.
04 — PlayStation 5
Supply Constraints, Remote Demand & the Restock Race

The PlayStation 5 launched in November 2020 at $499 MSRP into one of the worst supply environments in consumer electronics history. Sony's semiconductor supply was constrained by the same global chip shortage affecting GPU manufacturers, while demand was amplified by locked-down consumers with stimulus money and nowhere to spend it. Units retailed for $800–1,200 on secondary markets through 2021–2022. Restocks were sporadic, unpredictable, and sold out within minutes, making automated monitoring and alert systems essential to capturing inventory at retail price before bots cleared stock.

PlayStation 5, Secondary Market Price vs. MSRP (2021–2023)
Methodology: Restock Monitoring
PS5 restocks at major retailers (Best Buy, Target, Walmart) followed predictable patterns. Monitoring inventory endpoints and social signals allowed for advance positioning before public announcements.
Signal: Margin Compression (2023)
By mid-2023, Sony had resolved the bulk of its supply constraints. Secondary margins compressed from 60–80% premium to 5–10%, signaling a category exit point for resale-focused inventory.
05 — PSA-Graded Collectibles
Stimulus-Fueled Speculation & the Market Split

The collectibles market followed the same stimulus-driven pattern as electronics. Influencer coverage of Pokémon cards in early 2021, combined with millions of people at home with extra cash and no live entertainment, triggered a speculative wave that sent PSA-graded card prices up 200–400% from pre-pandemic levels. By 2023, the broad market had corrected 40–60% as speculators exited. However, the correction was not uniform: high-grade cards (PSA 9/10) in low-population, iconic sets retained value far better than bulk mid-grade inventory, showing that the market had matured and only quality inventory held its value.

PSA Collectibles Market, High-Grade vs. Broad Market Index (2020–2023, Base: 100)
Insight: Quality Holds Value
PSA 10 cards in low-pop sets declined only 10–15% from peak, while the broad market fell 50%+. Concentration in high-grade assets was the correct strategy entering the correction.
Risk: Bulk Inventory
Speculative buyers who held mid-grade common-set cards faced severe liquidity issues as the market cooled. Demand monitoring via sell-through data flagged this deterioration early.
06 — Strategic Takeaways
What the Data Indicated for H1 2023
Analyst Conclusion
Macro Timing + Real-Time Monitoring = Sourcing Edge

The core insight across all three categories was that macro signals and on-the-ground monitoring worked together, neither alone was sufficient. CPI and crypto data explained why pricing was where it was; social monitoring, search trends, and restock alerts determined when and where to act.

GPUs: The post-mining correction created a below-MSRP acquisition window. Gaming demand remained structurally intact, making this a clear buy signal for Q1 2023 with an expected recovery to 10–15% above MSRP within 60–90 days.

PS5: Supply normalization by mid-2023 shrank secondary margins to near zero. The category required exit or a pivot toward bundle configurations that maintained perceived value above commodity pricing.

Collectibles: Market maturation favored quality concentration. Monitoring sell-through velocity on PSA 10 low-pop cards showed resilience while bulk inventory flagged as a liquidation priority.
